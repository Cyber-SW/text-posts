### Tuesday Post:

My client's operations bot does what Slack's API forbids.

That bot has to live in every client and pipeline channel in Slack. Before I automated it, I added it to each new channel by hand.

But doing things by hand is so 2023.

The fix sounded simple. Watch the workspace, catch every new channel, filter for the relevant ones, invite the bot.

Then I hit the wall most Slack automations die on. Permissions.

The Slack API blocks bots from inviting themselves into private and shared channels. But those were the exact channels the bot existed to work in.

The workaround: invite the bot with a user token instead.

I used the owner's token. He is the one person guaranteed to sit in every one of those channels, so the moment a new one is created, the bot is added to it automatically.

Even though this isn't some super fancy workflow that saves 30 minutes a day, it takes a tedious task off my hands and prevents delays in a time when speed matters most.

### Thursday Post:

My client was paying his editors to serve customers who had stopped paying him.

He runs a subscription service. Editors produce video ads for his customers, and they get paid per finished video.

The work lives on Trello. One board per customer. As long as a board is open, editors keep picking up work, and keep getting paid.

Here is where the money leaked.

When a customer's payment failed, the board stayed open until someone noticed and closed the board. So editors kept producing videos for a customer who was no longer paying.

A board left open one extra week meant another batch of billable videos delivered for free. Depending on the plan, that gap cost him up to 560€ a week.

Now an n8n workflow watches the payment status directly. The moment a payment fails the third time, the board closes on its own and editors cannot pick up new work for that customer. 

Once the payment clears, the board reopens automatically.

Where is money leaking out of your business while nobody is watching?

### Friday Post:

Companies replaced their support teams with AI. 74% are quietly switching it back.

The reason is simple. 

Customer conversations need judgment, context, and someone who can read the room. 

That is the hardest thing to automate, and they automated it first.

The half that actually automates is the half nobody sees.

The back office. 

Onboarding a client across five tools.
Routing new leads to the right rep.
Chasing unpaid invoices.
Pulling reports every morning. 

None of it needs judgment. All of it runs on rules.

That work is boring, invisible, and it doesn't sound as cool as "My AI agent swarm runs my whole business on autopilot" (while the token bill is bigger than the team it replaced).

It is also exactly where automation brings the most value.

The companies winning with automation are not the ones who fired their support team. 

They are the ones who automated the back office and left the relationships to humans.

### Saturday Post:

The US government just cut access to Fable 5 and Mythos 5.

On June 12, a global export restriction went into effect.

Foreign critical-infrastructure operators in more than 15 countries lost access immediately. The EU's own cyber agency, ENISA, was one of them.

A friendly reminder that you should not rely on a single model provider.

And hopefully another wake-up call for Europe to not rely on US tech.

The model you build on is not your product. It is infrastructure you rent from a company that itself runs on government permission.

So build for the day it disappears:
→ Abstract the model layer so swapping providers is a config change.
→ Keep a second model wired and tested.
→ Know which workflows die once a model vanishes.

This incident shows that access can be cut at any time.
