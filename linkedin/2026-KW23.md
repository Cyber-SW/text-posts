### Monday Post:

This reddit post shows why 80% of AI projects fail to deliver business value.

Spoiler: it's not the tech.

So why did this project fail?

First, the scope. Making one agent responsible for multiple tasks, in this case data collection, email drafting, and scheduling, is a bad idea.

When it comes to accuracy, specialized single-task agents tend to outperform multipurpose ones. Connect them through the same CRM and, if needed, put an orchestration agent on top.

Second, the guardrails: "One of them kept pulling the wrong data source for two weeks before anyone noticed."

Deterministic checks that validate before every write or send operation, plus clean error logging, would have caught that.

Third, false expectations from management: "...my manager said we need to give the tools time to learn."

Many managers adopt AI because they feel behind, not to fix a specific problem or automate a specific workflow. 

They dump AI on the team and expect productivity to jump, without understanding what an LLM can actually do.

The tech was never the bottleneck. Scope, guardrails, and a clear goal are where most AI projects fail.

### Wednesday Post:

Most "AI content" is slop. Here's how to automate content properly.

AI gurus tell you how they automated their entire content production and now post every day without lifting a finger.

This is possible!

But what no guru tells you is that it won't get you views.

So how do you automate content production properly?

There is one simple rule.

Automate execution, not creativity.

Meaning: write the scripts yourself, in your voice. Automate the research, the reformatting, and the posting.

This way you can write one post, let AI adapt it to each platform, and auto-post it.

### Saturday Post:

I've built hundreds of workflows. Most of them turned out useless.

Over time, I developed a simple framework to make sure they actually deliver.

Before you build, answer these two questions:
1. Is it worth automating, in time or money?
2. Can you describe a successful outcome for every step of the workflow?

If you can't answer yes to both, you either don't understand the process yet, it has no clear structure, or it isn't worth automating.

If you can, build it:
3. Set the goal -> X hours or dollars saved in a set timeframe.
4. Write the success criteria for each step, then build to them.
5. Measure against the goal and harden it against edge cases until it runs like clockwork.

Skip this framework, and you just build another useless workflow.

### Sunday Post:

I stole Andrej Karpathy's personal knowledge base setup, and here's why you should too.

Karpathy was a founding member of OpenAI and now leads the group for early LLM training at Anthropic.

The setup is simple but brilliant:
→ raw/ is where articles, notes, transcripts, screenshots etc. goes, unsorted.
→ CLAUDE.md is the starting point of every session and includes, who I am and how I work.
→ index.md is the map so Claude always knows what exists and where to find it.
→ wiki/ is where Claude compiles the raw folder into clean, linked notes. It writes and maintains this by itself.
→ Obsidian is the frontend where Claude generates its outputs (different output formats are possible -> Marp format, matplotlib, etc.)
→ Run regular "health checks" over the wiki to cleanup or enrich the data
→ Enhance even further with custom vibecoded tools
